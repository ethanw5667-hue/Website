Climbing Magnets — a stick–slip investigation

IYPT Dynamics Magnetostatics Materials

We study why a horizontal rod of stacked neodymium magnets climbs a vertical ferromagnetic rod when the rod spins. The ascent emerges from a torque-driven stick–slip cycle along a shifting contact line. We couple a minimal dynamics model to measured interface friction and B–H hysteresis of candidate steels, then compare predicted vs observed climb trends.

1) Phenomenology — how ascent appears

At low RPM the magnet rod rests against the steel and sticks. As the steel rotates, tangential drag and off-axis magnetic attraction produce a growing torque about the instantaneous contact line. When the static-friction threshold is exceeded, the rod slips: it rotates a few degrees, the contact line shifts upward, and the rod gains a small vertical step. Repeating cycles yield a non-zero mean climb velocity. Step size and period depend on friction, field strength, steel permeability, and geometry.

Model idea. Treat the interface as a translating pressure profile. Stick while τ_mag(θ)+τ_g(θ) ≤ μ_s N r_eff; advance through slip with μk and update the vertical position. Averaging over one revolution gives climb rate vs RPM.

2) Stick–slip dynamics (minimal model)

  • Magnetostatics. Magnet stack ≈ line of dipoles; attraction to a finite-μ cylinder sets normal force and magnetic torque (dipole strength calibrated by pull tests).
  • Contact mechanics. Arc contact with pressure profile p(θ); rotation translates the profile and the torque lever arm.
  • Slip criterion. Transition when τ_mag(θ)+τ_g(θ) > μ_s N r_eff; evolve with kinetic friction during slip.
  • Integration. Euler step in stick; explicit update across slip; iterate per revolution to obtain mean vertical velocity.

3) Interface friction — measured without magnetic bias

Direct ramp tests are corrupted by magnetization. We removed magnetization by heating the magnets above Curie, then measured friction with the magnets inert.

  • Problem. Heating in air oxidized surfaces and changed μ.
  • Solution. Seal the magnet stack in a vacuum glass ampoule, heat above Curie to demagnetize, perform inclined-plane tests for μs, μk on different finishes. Afterward, remagnetize/replace magnets.
  • Outcome. Stable μ independent of field history; values fed directly into the model.
Bench during materials runs (friction and B–H characterization).

4) Materials characterization — B–H loops (9 steels)

Steel choice sets permeability, saturation, and coercivity, which alter N and τ_mag. We measured B–H loops for nine McMaster-Carr rods using a standard drive/pickup coil rig:

  • Primary (H): long solenoid driven by a sinusoid; a shunt resistor on CH1 gives current ∝ H.
  • Secondary (B): short pickup near mid-span; CH2 ≈ dB/dt; integrate/scale to B, or plot CH2 vs CH1 in XY mode at fixed frequency.
  • Output: full hysteresis loops → extract initial μ, coercive field, saturation trend for the model.
Concept: CH1 → shunt (H), CH2 → pickup (∝ dB/dt).

5) Results & discussion

  • Climb rate vs RPM. The model captures the measured trend; sensitivity shows μ and the approach to saturation are first-order parameters.
  • Instabilities. At edges/paint or strong curvature changes, slips may yield no net ascent—consistent with the torque threshold picture. A thin compliant layer smooths the contact-line motion.
  • Design implications. Control micro-gap/finish; choose steels with favorable B–H; add compliance to suppress non-productive slips.
Next. Add a Hall sensor for in-situ field/gap estimation and prototype a quick-release wedge to reduce peel force at lift-off.
