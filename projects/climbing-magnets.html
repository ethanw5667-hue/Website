Climbing Magnets — a stick–slip investigation

IYPT Dynamics Magnetostatics Materials

We study why a horizontal rod of stacked neodymium magnets climbs a vertical ferromagnetic rod when the rod spins. The observed ascent emerges from a torque-driven stick–slip cycle along a shifting contact line. We couple a minimal dynamics model to measured interface friction and B–H hysteresis of candidate steels, then compare predicted vs measured climb trends.

1) Phenomenology — how ascent appears

At low RPM the magnet rod rests against the steel and sticks. As the steel rotates, tangential drag and the off-axis magnetic attraction produce a growing torque about the instantaneous contact line. When the static-friction threshold is exceeded, the rod slips: it rotates a few degrees, the contact line shifts upward, and the rod gains a small vertical step. Repeated cycles yield a non-zero mean climb velocity. The step size and period depend on friction, field strength, steel permeability, and geometry.

Model idea. Treat the interface as a pressure profile that translates with rotation. Stick while τ_mag(θ)+τ_g(θ) ≤ μ_s N r_eff; integrate the rotational dynamics through the slip with kinetic friction to update the vertical position. Average over one revolution to predict climb rate vs RPM.

2) Stick–slip dynamics (minimal model)

  • Magnetostatics. Approximate the magnet stack as a line of dipoles; attraction to a finite-μ cylinder sets the normal force and magnetic torque. Calibrate dipole strength by pull tests.
  • Contact mechanics. Arc contact with pressure profile p(θ); as the cylinder rotates the profile translates, changing lever arms of τ_mag and the normal reaction.
  • Slip criterion. Transition at τ_mag(θ)+τ_g(θ) > μ_s N r_eff; evolve with μk during slip.
  • Integration. Euler step in stick; explicit update across slip; iterate per revolution to obtain mean vertical velocity.

3) Interface friction — measured without magnetic bias

Direct ramp tests are corrupted by magnetization. We therefore removed magnetization by heating the magnets above their Curie point, then measured friction with the magnets inert.

  • Problem. Heating in air oxidized surfaces and altered friction.
  • Solution. Seal the magnet stack in a vacuum glass ampoule, then heat above Curie to demagnetize. Conduct inclined-plane tests to estimate μs, μk for different steel finishes. After tests, remagnetize/replace magnets for operation.
  • Outcome. Stable μ values independent of field history; these feed directly into the dynamics model.

4) Materials characterization — B–H loops (9 steels)

Steel choice sets permeability, saturation, and coercivity, all of which affect N and τ_mag. We measured B–H loops for nine McMaster-Carr rods with a standard drive/pickup coil rig:

  • Primary (H): long solenoid driven by a sinusoid. A shunt resistor is monitored on scope CH1; current ∝ H.
  • Secondary (B): short pickup coil near mid-span. CH2 ≈ dB/dt; integrate/scale to obtain B. In practice we use fixed-frequency scaling and plot CH2 vs CH1 in XY mode.
  • Output: full hysteresis loops per sample; we extract initial permeability, saturation trend, and coercive field for the model.
Conceptual wiring: CH1 → shunt (H), CH2 → pickup (∝ dB/dt).
Overlay of the nine samples. Differences in initial slope and coercivity explain changes in normal force and the torque budget, hence climb rate.

5) Results & discussion

  • Climb rate vs RPM. The model captures the measured trend; sensitivity analysis shows μ and saturation approach are first-order parameters.
  • Instabilities. At edges/paint or large curvature changes, a slip event can yield no net ascent—consistent with the torque threshold picture. Adding a thin compliant layer smooths the contact-line motion.
  • Design implications. Control micro-gap and finish; choose steels with favorable B–H; manage compliance to suppress nonproductive slips.
Limitations & next steps. Quasi-2D magnetostatics and simplified pressure profiles ignore local asperities and eddy currents. Next, add a Hall sensor for in-situ field/gap estimation and prototype a quick-release wedge to reduce peel force at lift-off.
